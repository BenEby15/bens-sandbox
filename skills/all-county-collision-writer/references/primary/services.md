## **All County Collision: Services**

### **Purpose**

This internal document defines All County Collision’s service offering, repair workflow, operational strengths, supported work types, preferred job mix, and service-related messaging guidance for internal marketing use and Custom GPT training.

This is not public website copy. It is an internal reference document meant to help the marketing team and AI systems describe the shop’s services accurately, clearly, and consistently across web content, landing pages, GBP posts, and related materials. The onboarding document is the authority source for hard service facts, capabilities, tool references, and stated process details.

### **Service Overview**

All County Collision should be understood first and foremost as a **full-service collision repair shop**, not a general repair facility and not a specialty restoration shop. That distinction matters. Internally, the brand direction is clearly moving toward **All County Collision**, dropping “and Repair” because Ryan does not want the business framed like a general mechanical repair operation.

The service identity of the shop is built around proper collision repair planning, structural and cosmetic repair execution, modern vehicle system awareness, and a guided customer experience from intake through delivery. The business is set up to handle the normal breadth of collision-related repair needs for modern passenger vehicles, while also being selective about the kinds of work it does and does not want.

For marketing and AI purposes, the most important framing is this:

**All County Collision helps customers through the full collision repair process with careful planning, quality-focused repairs, and clear communication from start to finish.**

That sentence works because it reflects the real operational shape of the business. The shop does not just perform isolated body work. It manages an entire repair journey.

### **Main Service Categories**

The onboarding document identifies the shop’s main services as collision repair, ADAS work, mechanical support, fleet work, light in-house PDR, with glass replacement and most PDR sublet as needed.

Those service lines should be understood in the following way.

#### **Collision Repair**

Collision repair is the shop’s primary service and the center of its identity. This includes the full sequence of activities required to restore a damaged vehicle after an accident, from estimating and repair planning through disassembly, structural or body repair, refinishing, reassembly, scanning, calibration, and delivery.

This should not be reduced internally to “body work.” Ryan’s process description makes clear that the shop approaches repairs through a planned, managed sequence rather than a loose repair-as-you-go model. That is a real service differentiator and should shape how the marketing team talks about the shop.

#### **Structural Repair**

The onboarding document specifically identifies structural repair as part of the workflow when needed. This means the shop is not limited to purely cosmetic damage. The presence of a Chief S21 frame rack and Car-O-Liner Vision measuring system also supports a more capable structural positioning.

Marketing implication:  
 Structural repair can be referenced as part of the shop’s broader collision capability, but it should be described carefully and factually, not sensationally. The best framing is that the shop has the tools and process to address structural damage when a repair calls for it.

#### **Sheet Metal and Parts Replacement**

The service process explicitly includes sheet metal work and parts replacement. This matters because it reflects normal collision repair reality and supports content around proper fit, replacement decisions, and repair planning.

The transcript also adds useful context: Ryan described technicians receiving vehicles fully disassembled with parts received, mirror-matched, and prepared so body work can proceed in a controlled way. That helps explain that parts replacement at All County Collision is not handled casually. It is integrated into the shop’s broader planning and QC process.

#### **Refinishing and Paint**

Prep and paint are built into the core service process, supported by two heated Garmat downdraft booths, Axalta Cromax XP paint, and Irus Scan paint matching tools.

This should be treated as a major customer-facing service theme because one of the most common customer questions is whether the color will match. That means paint and color match content are not just technical support topics. They are directly tied to customer trust.

Marketing implication:  
 Color match reassurance should be one of the shop’s strongest recurring service-support themes across GBP posts, FAQ content, and service pages.

#### **ADAS and Scanning**

ADAS work is performed in-house, and the documented workflow includes pre-scan, post-scan, and ADAS repair planning. The facility and tooling list also includes Autel scanning and diagnostics plus the Autel IA900 for ADAS recalibration.

This is a major service strength and should be treated as one of the shop’s strongest trust builders for newer-model vehicles. It supports the idea that All County Collision is equipped for modern repairs, not just older-style cosmetic body work.

Marketing implication:  
 ADAS and scanning should not dominate every page, but whenever the content is about newer vehicles, proper repairs, safety systems, or OEM procedures, this service line becomes an important supporting proof point.

#### **Mechanical Work**

Mechanical work is described as a blend of in-house and sublet support. This means All County Collision should not be marketed like a full general mechanical shop, but it also should not be described as incapable of handling mechanical needs that arise within a collision repair context.

The cleanest internal positioning is:

* collision-related mechanical needs can be addressed as part of the repair process  
* some mechanical work is handled in-house  
* some is coordinated through trusted sublet partners when needed

This wording is accurate, flexible, and aligned with the brand direction.

#### **Paintless Dent Repair**

PDR is a blended service line. The onboarding document says PDR is primarily sublet, with light PDR performed in-house. The transcript expands on this by noting that the shop invested in glue pull products and can handle lighter PDR internally.

This matters because it creates a useful distinction:

* All County Collision can speak credibly about PDR  
* but it should not overstate PDR as a dominant specialty if that is not the actual operating model

Marketing implication:  
 PDR can be included in service lists and FAQs, but the shop should not be positioned as a dedicated PDR-only specialist.

#### **Glass Replacement**

Glass replacement is sublet. This is simple but important. It means the shop can help coordinate glass needs as part of a broader collision repair, but it should not be marketed as primarily an in-house glass replacement center.

#### **Fleet Work**

Fleet work is explicitly listed as a yes, and Ryan states clearly in the transcript that he wants fleet work. This makes fleet one of the more commercially important service categories for future content and lead strategy.

Marketing implication:  
 Fleet should be treated as a legitimate growth-oriented service line, not a throwaway bullet point. That does not mean it needs to be foregrounded on every page, but it is worth dedicated future content, especially if the team later builds targeted landing pages or commercial outreach material.

### **How the Repair Process Actually Works**

One of All County Collision’s strongest service advantages is that it has a clearly defined process, not just a list of repair capabilities. The onboarding document gives a detailed start-to-finish workflow. That workflow is important because it helps the marketing team explain the repair journey in a way that builds trust.

#### **Before drop-off**

The process begins with either:

* an in-person inspection, or  
* a photo estimate workflow

If the vehicle is not drivable, the shop may take it in right away. If it is drivable, the shop works to pre-manage the repair by writing a preliminary estimate, working through approvals, and ordering parts in advance where possible.

That is a strong operational detail because it shows the shop is trying to reduce friction and prepare before the car is physically in the building for full production.

#### **Intake and pre-repair stage**

Once the vehicle is dropped off, the shop helps coordinate rental replacement, offers after-hours drop-off via a drop box, checks the vehicle in, pre-washes it, records job information, and moves the vehicle into pre-scan and ADAS repair planning before disassembly for damage discovery.

This stage matters because it reflects order and control. The repair is not treated like a casual handoff. It is treated like a managed intake.

#### **Repair planning and approvals**

After disassembly and damage discovery, the shop works through approvals with both the customer and insurance when applicable, then moves into parts procurement, mirror matching, and test fitting.

This is especially useful for website and FAQ content because it helps explain why the first visible estimate is not always the full story, why hidden damage matters, and why a repair timeline can evolve after disassembly.

#### **Body, structural, and refinish stages**

During production, the vehicle moves through body repair, sheet metal work, structural repair when required, then through department handoff QC into prep and paint, followed by another QC stop before reassembly.

This sequence is important because it gives real substance to the shop’s “multiple quality control checkpoints” claim. That differentiator is not separate from services. It is part of how the services are delivered.

#### **Post-repair and delivery**

After paint and reassembly, the process may include glass, mechanical, and alignment steps as needed, then post-scan and ADAS work, detailing, final QC, and often a test drive before pickup.

This is an especially strong service narrative because it shows the repair is not considered complete at the moment paint dries. The shop has a full finishing and verification phase before delivery.

### **Service Philosophy: Correct Repair, Not Shortcut Repair**

All County Collision’s service positioning is not just about what it does. It is also about **how** it does it.

The onboarding document states that the shop carefully follows OEM procedures, keeps customers informed, and will not sacrifice quality or skip steps for speed or money. That means the services should always be framed through a quality lens.

The transcript reinforces this in plainer terms. Ryan talks about not bending to insurer pressure, not rushing repairs just to hit cost and speed metrics, and keeping the customer involved in decisions about how their vehicle is repaired. That is not just brand language. It is part of the service philosophy.

For internal use, this means service descriptions should consistently imply:

* repair planning matters  
* proper procedures matter  
* customer communication is part of the service  
* quality control is part of the service  
* scans and calibrations matter for newer vehicles  
* timelines and costs should be handled honestly, not overpromised

### **Parts Policy and Service Messaging**

The onboarding document includes a parts policy that emphasizes restoring the vehicle to original factory condition and performance using manufacturer-branded or OEM-approved parts, while discussing alternatives with the customer if needed and staying within state law.

This is important because parts are not just a legal or operational detail. They are part of how the shop’s services should be represented.

Marketing implication:  
 When content touches on parts, the shop should be positioned as prioritizing OEM quality and safety, while being transparent with customers about their options. Content should avoid rigid or overly absolute phrasing unless the specific context calls for it.

### **Warranty as a Service Support Point**

The onboarding document describes a limited lifetime warranty on repairs and paint for as long as the customer owns the vehicle, with parts covered according to the manufacturer’s warranty.

Warranty should not be the lead message on every service page, but it is an important service-support trust signal. It works especially well in:

* FAQ pages  
* why choose us content  
* landing pages built around trust and reassurance  
* customer journey content

### **What All County Collision Does Not Want to Be Known For**

One of the most useful parts of the onboarding material is the section on jobs the shop does **not** want. That list includes:

* classic restorations  
* rust repair  
* partial repairs  
* semi-truck or oversized work  
* boats, RVs, and fiberglass-heavy work

This matters strategically. A good internal service document is not just about what the shop does. It also protects the shop from attracting the wrong work mix.

For internal guidance, Custom GPT should avoid drifting into language that makes All County Collision sound like:

* a restoration shop  
* a rust specialist  
* a heavy truck repair center  
* an RV or marine refinishing shop  
* a general fiberglass repair destination

That would create bad lead quality and weaken the brand.

### **Ideal Service Fit**

The onboarding material identifies the ideal audience as people who care about the safety and quality of their vehicle, especially owners of newer-model vehicles. This fits closely with the shop’s service strengths.

The best-fit service customer is likely someone who:

* has collision damage on a modern passenger vehicle  
* values proper repair standards  
* wants good communication  
* is concerned about safety systems, color match, and repair quality  
* wants guidance through a stressful process  
* does not simply want the lowest-price shortcut

This should influence how future service pages are written. The strongest service messaging will usually speak to trust, clarity, and doing the job right.

### **FAQ and Content Opportunities Around Services**

The onboarding document gives very strong guidance here. The most common customer questions are:

* Is the color going to match?  
* Do you work with my insurance company?  
* How much is this going to cost me out of pocket?  
* How long is it going to take?

The questions customers should ask, but often do not, include:

* Will my vehicle be repaired with all OEM parts?  
* Will you follow OEM repair procedures?  
* What is your warranty and what does limited mean?  
* Why might I owe more than my deductible?

These are excellent service-content topics because they tie directly to customer concerns and the shop’s strongest trust signals.

### **Internal GPT Guidance for Service Content**

When the Custom GPT writes about services for All County Collision, it should:

* describe the shop as a collision-focused business, not a general repair shop  
* lead with quality, communication, and repair process clarity  
* mention OEM procedures when relevant  
* use ADAS, scanning, and calibration language especially for newer vehicle content  
* explain service steps in plain language  
* avoid overstating in-house scope where the work is actually blended or sublet  
* treat fleet as a real service line  
* avoid attracting restoration, heavy truck, RV, boat, or fiberglass-heavy work  
* keep Eustis as the anchor location unless a prompt specifically targets another area

### **Summary**

All County Collision’s services should be understood as a **managed, quality-focused collision repair offering** built around proper repair planning, modern vehicle awareness, and customer guidance from intake to delivery.

The shop’s service identity is strongest when framed through:

* full collision repair capability  
* structured repair planning  
* multiple QC checkpoints  
* OEM-aware procedures  
* in-house ADAS capability  
* blended mechanical and PDR support  
* coordinated sublet support where appropriate  
* honest selectivity about the work the shop does and does not want

For internal use, the main takeaway is this: All County Collision does not just fix damaged vehicles. It manages collision repairs in a controlled, customer-guiding way that reflects the shop’s deeper standards around quality, safety, and communication.
